Understanding Goldbach Conjectures

The Goldbach Conjectures are two of the oldest and best-known unsolved problems in number theory and mathematics. They propose properties of prime numbers and their sums.

Strong Goldbach Conjecture

Also known as the binary Goldbach Conjecture, it states that: "Every even integer greater than 2 is the sum of two prime numbers." For example, 4 = 2 + 2, 6 = 3 + 3, 8 = 3 + 5, 10 = 3 + 7 or 5 + 5. This conjecture has been tested for all even numbers up to 4 × 1018 and holds true, but a formal mathematical proof remains elusive.

Weak Goldbach Conjecture

Also known as the ternary Goldbach Conjecture, it states that: "Every odd integer greater than 5 can be expressed as the sum of three prime numbers." For example, 7 = 2 + 2 + 3, 9 = 3 + 3 + 3, 11 = 3 + 3 + 5. The weak Goldbach Conjecture was proven by Harald Helfgott in 2013, making it no longer an open problem. However, it's considered "weak" because if the strong conjecture is true, then the weak conjecture would automatically be true (since an odd number n > 5 can be written as n = (n-3) + 3, and if n-3 is an even number greater than 2, it can be written as p₁ + p₂, thus n = p₁ + p₂ + 3).
